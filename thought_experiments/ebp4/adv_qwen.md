# No Direct Testability: An Epistemic Audit of Copenhagen and Bohmian Mechanics in the Context of DESI's Cosmological Observations

## The Observational-Inference Pipeline: From DESI Data to Model Constraints

The path from raw astronomical data to profound conclusions about the nature of dark energy is a multi-layered process of measurement, modeling, and statistical inference. Understanding this pipeline is essential before attempting to layer philosophical interpretations of quantum mechanics onto the results. The Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI), one of the premier tools for precision cosmology, operates within this well-defined scientific framework [[11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.14745)]. Its primary objective is to place precise constraints on the equation of state of dark energy, the enigmatic force driving the accelerated expansion of the universe [[11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.14745), [74](https://arxiv.org/html/2504.15635v3)]. The instrument achieves this by measuring the redshifts of an immense number of celestial objects; in just three years of operation, DESI had measured redshifts for over 30 million galaxies and quasars [[53](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.14738v1)]. This vast dataset allows researchers to construct a detailed three-dimensional map of the large-scale structure of the cosmos [[12](https://academic.oup.com/mnras/article/547/2/staf2207/8378090)].

From this complex map, scientists extract a specific, statistically significant feature known as Baryon Acoustic Oscillations (BAO) [[27](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.26144v1), [102](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.11432v2)]. These oscillations are the frozen remnants of sound waves that propagated through the hot, dense plasma of the early universe [[8](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2509.01162)]. They imprinted a characteristic scale—a preferred separation distance between galaxies—into the matter distribution. This scale acts as a "standard ruler," a physical length whose apparent size in our sky today depends on the geometry and expansion history of the universe since the big bang [[11](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.14745)]. By precisely measuring the angular diameter distance and the Hubble parameter at various redshifts using the BAO feature, DESI provides crucial data points that can be used to test cosmological models [[54](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.17342v3), [55](https://escholarship.org/content/qt8xn4v0s5/qt8xn4v0s5.pdf)]. Recent analyses have incorporated BAO measurements from DESI's second data release (DR2) into their datasets, highlighting the importance of these observations in constraining the properties of dark energy [[27](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.26144v1), [28](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.06929v2), [55](https://escholarship.org/content/qt8xn4v0s5/qt8xn4v0s5.pdf)].

The final step in this pipeline is model inference, where the observed distances derived from BAO are compared against the theoretical predictions of competing cosmological models. The standard model of cosmology, known as the ΛCDM model, posits that the universe is dominated by cold dark matter (CDM) and a mysterious form of energy with a constant density, represented by Einstein's cosmological constant (Λ) [[63](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Cosmological-Parameter-Inference-with-Bayesian-Padilla-Tellez/bf292115f0eb7fba8d67621f602500cc967a5c80), [107](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.01814)]. In this model, dark energy has an equation of state parameter $w = P/\rho c^2$ equal to -1, meaning its energy density remains constant as the universe expands [[59](https://escholarship.org/content/qt1946q35d/qt1946q35d.pdf)]. However, the recent hints from DESI suggest a potential deviation from this simple picture, strengthening the case for models with a dynamical dark energy component [[46](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/395721832_DESI_Observations_and_the_Earlier_Predictions_of_Norma_G_Sanchez_Evolving_Dark_Energy_Vacuum_Energyt_solves_the_cosmological_constant_problem), [80](https://physics.stackexchange.com/questions/859844/is-there-a-consensus-about-the-implications-of-the-desi-results)]. These alternative models include quintessence, which proposes that dark energy is a rolling scalar field permeating space-time, leading to an equation of state that can evolve over time [[34](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2502.08876), [61](https://arxiv.org/html/2606.02701v1)]. Other possibilities include modifications to the theory of gravity itself, such as f(Q) symmetric teleparallel gravity, which could produce similar observational effects without invoking a new energy component [[96](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-4434/14/2/19)]. To determine which model best fits the data, cosmologists rely heavily on statistical frameworks, most notably Bayesian inference [[5](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2403.15526), [19](https://arxiv.org/html/2603.15585v2)]. This approach systematically compares the ability of different models to explain the data while penalizing unnecessary complexity, providing a rigorous method for model selection and parameter estimation [[65](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/352807303_Cosmological_Parameter_Inference_with_Bayesian_Statistics), [66](https://www.mdpi.com/2218-1997/7/7/213)]. The tension in modern cosmology arises when data from different probes, such as galaxy clustering and weak lensing, show slight discrepancies, suggesting that even the best-fit parameters may depend on the dataset being used [[64](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.12004v1), [69](https://arxiv.org/html/2308.16183v2)]. This entire process—from redshift measurement to BAO detection and Bayesian model comparison—is the bedrock upon which all subsequent scientific claims about dark energy are built. It is a process grounded in general relativity, statistical physics, and observational astronomy, operating on scales vastly larger than those typically associated with quantum mechanical phenomena. Any discussion of quantum interpretations must begin by acknowledging this established and successful framework, and critically evaluating whether such interpretations introduce changes at any point in this chain of reasoning.

## Ontological Frameworks: A Comparative Analysis of Copenhagen and Bohmian Mechanics

Before assessing the implications of quantum interpretations for cosmology, it is imperative to clarify their distinct ontological commitments—their views on what constitutes fundamental reality. The two most prominent interpretations discussed in this context, the Copenhagen interpretation and Bohmian mechanics, offer profoundly different answers to the question of what the mathematical formalism of quantum mechanics describes. The Copenhagen interpretation, often associated with Niels Bohr and Werner Heisenberg, is fundamentally an instrumentalist or pragmatist view [[31](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/356401118_Truth_by_fiat_the_Copenhagen_Interpretation_of_Quantum_Mechanics), [76](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qt-issues/)]. It does not seek to provide a literal, "pictorial" representation of microscopic reality. Instead, it focuses on the practical purpose of quantum mechanics: to serve as a tool for coordinating experience and forming expectations about the outcomes of experiments [[76](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qt-issues/)]. A central tenet, articulated by Bohr, is the necessity of using classical concepts—such as position, momentum, and time—to describe experimental arrangements and report results [[29](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qm-copenhagen/)]. He argued that regardless of how much quantum phenomena transcend classical explanation, the account of all evidence must be expressed in classical terms for unambiguous communication [[29](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qm-copenhagen/)]. This leads to the principle of complementarity, which posits that certain pairs of descriptions, like wave-like and particle-like behavior, are mutually exclusive yet complementary; observing one precludes observing the other, but both are necessary for a complete understanding of quantum systems [[29](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qm-copenhagen/)]. Crucially, Bohr viewed the wave function (ψ-function) as a symbolic entity, not as a physical field existing in real space. Consequently, he rejected the notion of a literal "collapse of the wave function" upon measurement, arguing instead that the act of measurement establishes the conditions under which classical concepts can be meaningfully applied to an object [[29](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qm-copenhagen/)]. This perspective effectively dissolves the measurement problem by shifting it from a physical process to an epistemological boundary between the quantum system and the classical measuring apparatus [[29](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qm-copenhagen/)]. Some proponents of this view argue that the Copenhagen interpretation is inapplicable to quantum cosmology, as the entire universe cannot be treated as a system under observation from an external frame [[117](https://www.mdpi.com/2218-1997/6/9/128)].

In stark contrast, Bohmian mechanics, also known as the de Broglie-Bohm pilot-wave theory, is a realist hidden-variable theory [[76](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qt-issues/)]. It posits that the standard quantum formalism is incomplete and requires additional variables to provide a complete description of physical reality [[76](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qt-issues/)]. Specifically, it asserts that particles possess definite positions and momenta at all times, guided by a "pilot wave" represented by the wave function [[30](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.18930), [76](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qt-issues/)]. Unlike the Copenhagen interpretation, which treats the wave function as non-real, Bohmian mechanics takes the ψ-function as a real, physical field that exists in a high-dimensional configuration space [[29](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qm-copenhagen/)]. The motion of each particle is determined by a "guiding equation" that depends on the instantaneous positions of all other particles, making the theory explicitly non-local [[71](https://www.mdpi.com/2073-8994/18/6/1016)]. This deterministic framework provides a clear resolution to the measurement problem: there is no discontinuous collapse of the wave function. Instead, interaction with the environment causes the wave function to split into non-interfering branches, each containing a pointer-like state of the measuring device correlated with a definite outcome for the system being measured [[76](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/qt-issues/)]. While this provides a compelling ontological picture, the theory faces significant technical challenges, particularly in formulating a version that is fully compatible with special and general relativity [[71](https://www.mdpi.com/2073-8994/18/6/1016)]. Some exploratory work has proposed a framework for Bohmian mechanics on curved spacetime, deriving novel experimental predictions, but a universally accepted and empirically verified formulation remains elusive [[71](https://www.mdpi.com/2073-8994/18/6/1016)]. The fundamental difference, therefore, lies in their ontological stance: Copenhagen is agnostic about underlying reality, focusing on operational predictions, while Bohmian mechanics provides a detailed, albeit complex and non-local, picture of particles moving deterministically within a guiding field. Neither interpretation, in its conventional application, modifies the Schrödinger equation or introduces new fields that would directly alter the laws of general relativity governing the cosmos.

## Empirical Testability: The Disconnect Between Quantum Foundations and Cosmological Probes

The central question of this analysis is whether the choice between the Copenhagen and Bohmian interpretations of quantum mechanics alters any empirical predictions derived from the standard models used to interpret DESI's observations of dark energy. The answer, based on the provided materials and the current state of physics, is a definitive no. There is currently no direct empirical testability for either interpretation in the context of DESI's cosmological probes. The mathematical formalisms of these interpretations operate at fundamentally different scales and domains from those of general relativity and observational cosmology. Quantum mechanics governs the behavior of microscopic systems like atoms and subatomic particles, while DESI measures the large-scale structure of the universe, a domain described by Einstein's theory of gravity. There is no established, empirically verified mechanism by which the choice of a quantum interpretation—be it the pragmatic symbolism of Copenhagen or the deterministic realism of Bohmian mechanics—would manifest as a detectable difference in the observables that DESI measures, such as galaxy clustering, the baryon acoustic oscillation peak position, or the overall expansion rate of the universe.

The logical pathway from a quantum interpretation to a cosmological prediction is long, circuitous, and entirely speculative. One might speculate that the origin of dark energy, often linked to the energy of the quantum vacuum, could be interpreted differently under each framework. For instance, a realist interpretation like Bohmian mechanics might imply a different definition of the vacuum state or a different way in which vacuum fluctuations contribute to the stress-energy tensor, which in turn sources spacetime curvature according to the Einstein field equations. Conversely, the Copenhagen interpretation, with its focus on measurement and classical concepts, might treat the vacuum energy calculation as purely a calculational artifact without direct physical sourcing power. However, this line of reasoning ventures far beyond established physics. The cosmological constant problem—the vast discrepancy between the theoretically predicted vacuum energy density and the observed dark energy density—is a profound open question in physics, but it is not a problem that has been solved or even significantly clarified by appealing to the Copenhagen or Bohmian interpretations [[88](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Cosmic-clues%3A-DESI%2C-dark-energy%2C-and-the-constant-Yin/55bdf193ab0eedc575914000d717a24125389b41), [91](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.08659v1)]. Researchers exploring solutions to this problem work within the frameworks of quantum field theory, string theory, or modified gravity, not by choosing a specific interpretation of quantum mechanics [[60](https://www.mdpi.com/2073-8994/17/6/888), [96](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-4434/14/2/19)]. Similarly, attempts to explain the DESI hints of evolving dark energy involve constructing new physical models, such as scalar field quintessence models or modified gravity theories, none of which are direct consequences of adopting Bohmian mechanics or Copenhagen [[34](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2502.08876), [61](https://arxiv.org/html/2606.02701v1)]. While some papers explore connections between scalar fields and the cosmological constant problem, these remain within the realm of conventional particle physics and effective field theory, not as unique predictions of Bohmian mechanics [[47](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/381031598_Cosmic_clues_DESI_dark_energy_and_the_cosmological_constant_problem), [78](https://arxiv.org/abs/2404.06444)].

Therefore, the claim that "Bohmian mechanics explains the DESI hints for evolving dark energy" is a category error. It incorrectly assumes that a foundational debate in quantum theory has direct, observable consequences for the large-scale dynamics of the universe as described by general relativity. Such a leap bypasses the entire model-inference pipeline, from the measurement of redshifts to the Bayesian comparison of cosmological models. The empirical content of DESI's data is exhausted by comparing observations against the predictions of well-defined models like ΛCDM and wCDM. Within this framework, the choice of a quantum interpretation is irrelevant because it does not change the predictions of the models themselves. The hint of evolving dark energy is a statistical tension that may require a new physical model (e.g., a new field or modification of gravity), but it does not, by itself, demand a re-evaluation of the foundational principles of quantum mechanics. Any attempt to connect them requires the construction of a novel, hybrid theory of quantum gravity and cosmology, a task that is far from completion and not supported by any evidence in the provided sources. Until such a theory is developed and makes new, testable predictions that go beyond those of general relativity and standard cosmology, the empirical testability of the Copenhagen and Bohmian interpretations in the context of DESI's observations remains zero.

## The Cosmological Constant Problem: A Cautious Synthesis of Speculative Links

The cosmological constant problem stands as one of the most significant unsolved puzzles in theoretical physics, distinct from, yet related to, the question of whether dark energy evolves [[88](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Cosmic-clues%3A-DESI%2C-dark-energy%2C-and-the-constant-Yin/55bdf193ab0eedc575914000d717a24125389b41)]. It concerns the staggering discrepancy—estimated to be around 120 orders of magnitude—between the value of the cosmological constant (or dark energy density) inferred from cosmological observations and the value naively calculated from quantum field theory vacuum fluctuations [[88](https://www.semanticscholar.org/paper/Cosmic-clues%3A-DESI%2C-dark-energy%2C-and-the-constant-Yin/55bdf193ab0eedc575914000d717a24125389b41)]. Addressing this problem is a primary motivation for many beyond-Standard-Model theories. The user's directive mandates a cautious approach, forbidding any conflation of the problem of the cosmological constant's small value with the problem of explaining DESI's hints for evolving dark energy. While both deal with dark energy, they are logically independent questions. A theory that successfully explains why the cosmological constant is so small does not necessarily predict that it must be evolving, and a model that accounts for evolving dark energy does not automatically resolve the fine-tuning issue of its present-day value.

Standard approaches to alleviating the cosmological constant problem fall outside the scope of quantum interpretations. For instance, dynamical dark energy models, such as quintessence, replace the static cosmological constant with a slowly evolving scalar field [[61](https://arxiv.org/html/2606.02701v1), [87](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.21875)]. Such models can help explain why dark energy became dominant recently by allowing the field's potential energy to roll to a small value at late times. However, they do not solve the core fine-tuning problem of why the field's potential minimum is so close to zero [[61](https://arxiv.org/html/2606.02701v1)]. Other proposals involve modifying gravity on cosmological scales, which could potentially explain cosmic acceleration without introducing a new energy component at all, thereby sidestepping the need to explain its small value [[96](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-4434/14/2/19)]. Phenomenological models of a decaying cosmological constant, Λ(t), have also been explored in light of DESI DR2 data, but these are extensions of general relativity rather than consequences of a specific quantum interpretation [[108](https://arxiv.org/html/2604.16564v1)].

Speculatively connecting quantum interpretations to the cosmological constant problem requires imagining a scenario where the foundational rules of quantum theory influence the calculation of vacuum energy. One could hypothesize that a realist interpretation like Bohmian mechanics, with its emphasis on definite particle positions and a real pilot wave, might lead to a different regularization or renormalization procedure for calculating vacuum energy compared to the operator-based formalism of standard quantum field theory, which is often associated with the pragmatic, instrumentalist spirit of the Copenhagen school. Such a difference could, in principle, alter the calculated energy density. However, this remains pure speculation. No concrete, peer-reviewed model has been presented in the provided sources that demonstrates how to perform such a calculation consistently or shows that it yields a result that aligns with observation and also explains the DESI data. The exploration of quantum gravity, such as in string theory or loop quantum gravity, represents the frontier where such questions might eventually be addressed, but these are highly active and unresolved areas of research [[60](https://www.mdpi.com/2073-8994/17/6/888)]. The provided materials contain no evidence to support the claim that a specific quantum interpretation offers a ready-made solution to the cosmological constant problem that can then be applied to explain DESI's results. Therefore, any argument that uses the DESI hints as evidence for a particular quantum interpretation, or that uses a favored interpretation to claim a solution to the cosmological constant problem, would be making a premature and unjustified leap, accumulating massive evidential debt according to any responsible epistemic framework.

## Epistemic Governance with the Elephant Bridge Protocol v2.1

To navigate the complex landscape of ambiguous frontier data and avoid unwarranted speculation, the Elephant Bridge Protocol v2.1 (EBP) serves as a crucial epistemic governance framework. As stipulated, EBP is not a physical theory but a meta-level discipline for auditing the quality and responsibility of scientific claims [[51](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/402763416_ERES_INSTITUTE_FOR_NEW_AGE_CYBERNETICS_RESOLUTION_PROTOCOL_Formal_Specification_for_Multi-Domain_Dispute_Resolution_Governance_Adjudication_and_Systemic_Reconciliation_within_the_New_Age_Cybernetics_F), [81](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.00675)]. Its purpose is to classify claims based on their evidential support, expose the "debt" incurred by promoting a claim prematurely, and enforce rigorous standards of validation before a claim is elevated to a higher level of promotion [[97](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2410.13042)]. Applying EBP to the topic of DESI and quantum interpretations provides a systematic way to police the transition from cautious observation to bold, speculative conclusion, punishing any answer that jumps directly from DESI hints to "Bohmian mechanics explains dark energy" without passing through the required layers of model inference and validation.

The protocol's key concepts, such as `needNullModel`, `needObstruction`, and `needFaithfulnessReview`, are directly applicable here. A low-debt claim would be one firmly rooted in the observational-inference layer, such as: "DESI DR2 results strengthen hints of a deviation from the dark energy equation of state $w = -1$" [[46](https://www.researchgate.net/publication/395721832_DESI_Observations_and_the_Earlier_Predictions_of_Norma_G_Sanchez_Evolving_Dark_Energy_Vacuum_Energyt_solves_the_cosmological_constant_problem), [73](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.14743v1)]. This statement is grounded in the data, acknowledges the tentative nature of the finding, and aligns with the measured statistical tension between the data and the ΛCDM null model [[12](https://academic.oup.com/mnras/article/547/2/staf2207/8378090), [33](https://arxiv.org/html/2504.15336v2)]. It passes several EBP checks: it implicitly references a null model (ΛCDM), it avoids obstructions by not mixing unrelated domains, and it is a faithful summary of the reported results [[32](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.08056v3)]. In contrast, a high-debt claim like "Bohmian mechanics explains the DESI hints for evolving dark energy" fails multiple critical checks. It violates `needNullModel` by failing to compare itself against alternative explanations like quintessence or modified gravity [[34](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2502.08876), [96](https://www.mdpi.com/2075-4434/14/2/19)]. It incurs `needObstruction` debt because it deliberately bypasses the entire established model-inference pipeline, creating an artificial link between two unrelated domains of physics [[17](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.10713v1)]. Furthermore, it accumulates enormous `needMap` debt, as it provides no mapping from Bohmian concepts (e.g., pilot waves, particle positions) to the cosmological parameters being constrained by DESI (e.g., $w(a)$) [[79](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.17342)]. Without such a mapping, the claim is empty of scientific content.

By treating ontology and epistemology as separate threads, EBP provides a powerful audit mechanism. The **ontology thread** asks what a given theory says about reality: What are the entities and relationships posited by ΛCDM? What are the commitments of the Copenhagen interpretation versus Bohmian mechanics? The **epistemic-governance thread** asks how responsibly a claim about reality is being made: What evidence supports it? What alternatives have been ruled out? How was the evidence obtained and analyzed? The synthesis occurs at the audit layer, where the ontological commitments of a theory inform its proof obligations. A realist theory like Bohmian mechanics, which posits a more complex underlying reality, carries a heavier burden of proof to demonstrate how its ontology connects to observable phenomena. EBP makes this burden explicit. Before a claim that "Bohmian mechanics explains DESI" can be promoted, it would need to pass a battery of tests including `needNullModel` (showing it fits the data better than ΛCDM), `needInvariant` (demonstrating its core predictions are independent of coordinate choices), `needToyCheck` (validating its logic on simplified models), `needMap` (connecting pilot-wave concepts to cosmological parameters), `needObstruction` (addressing why this connection is not obvious or has been missed), and `needFaithfulnessReview` (ensuring all sources are cited correctly) [[97](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2410.13042)]. The failure to meet these requirements is not a dismissal of the idea, but a clear signal from the governance framework that the evidential debt is too high and the claim is premature. This disciplined approach ensures that scientific discourse progresses not by the attractiveness of a philosophical idea, but by the weight of the evidence supporting it.

## Synthesis and Audit: Navigating Claims About Dark Energy and Quantum Reality

This comprehensive analysis, conducted under the strict guidance of the research goal and the epistemic governance principles of the Elephant Bridge Protocol v2.1, reveals a clear and decisive conclusion. The primary directive was to evaluate the empirical testability of the Copenhagen and Bohmian interpretations in the context of DESI's observations of dark energy. The investigation confirms that, at present, there is no direct empirical testability. Neither interpretation alters the fundamental equations or the empirical predictions of the cosmological models—such as ΛCDM or dynamical dark energy models—that are used to infer the properties of dark energy from DESI's data on baryon acoustic oscillations. The path from DESI's measurement of galaxy redshifts to the statistical inference of an evolving dark energy equation of state is a journey through the well-established frameworks of general relativity and Bayesian statistics. It does not, in its current form, intersect with the foundational debates of quantum theory. Therefore, any claim suggesting that a specific quantum interpretation provides a direct explanation for DESI's hints of evolving dark energy is a premature speculation that accumulates significant evidential debt.

The analysis proceeded by first establishing the distinct layers of inquiry, as prescribed. The **observational-inference layer** was clarified, detailing the process from DESI's redshift surveys to the extraction of the BAO standard ruler and the subsequent Bayesian comparison of models. Only after this foundation was laid was the **quantum interpretation layer** examined, contrasting the instrumentalist, agnostic ontology of the Copenhagen interpretation with the realist, deterministic ontology of Bohmian mechanics. The core insight from this comparison is that these interpretations offer different philosophies of science but do not, in their conventional applications, generate different physical predictions for cosmological observables. The distinction between them is currently a matter of philosophy, not empirical science, as neither ontology affects the fit of models to the DESI data.

Furthermore, the analysis adhered to the cautious directive regarding the cosmological constant problem. It was established that this problem, concerning the extreme smallness of the observed dark energy density, is logically distinct from the question of whether dark energy evolves. While some dynamical models can alleviate aspects of the former, no evidence was found in the provided sources to support the claim that a particular quantum interpretation offers a unique or validated solution to the cosmological constant problem that also explains the DESI hints. The connection remains speculative and requires a novel, unverified theory of quantum gravity.

Finally, the **Elephant Bridge Protocol v2.1** served as the epistemic audit framework, synthesizing the ontological and epistemological threads. A high-debt claim is one that ignores these boundaries, fabricating connections without a valid mapping or comparison to null models. In conclusion, the current state of knowledge, based on the available evidence, dictates a strict separation of these domains. The hints from DESI are a challenge for cosmologists to refine their models of dark energy, not for physicists to re-examine the foundations of quantum mechanics. Any future synthesis will require a bridge—a new physical theory—something that does not currently exist in the literature reviewed here.
