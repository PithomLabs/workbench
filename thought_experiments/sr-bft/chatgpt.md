## The core idea

In ordinary relativity, there is no absolute simultaneity. “Now” depends on the observer’s frame.

But Bohmian mechanics has a problem: its guidance law is deeply **nonlocal**. The actual configuration of one system can depend on the configuration of distant systems entangled with it. That creates pressure: to define “the actual configuration of the universe,” Bohmian theories often seem to need something like a preferred slicing of spacetime — a foliation — even if that slicing is hidden from ordinary experiments. Relativistic Bohmian models commonly introduce such a privileged foliation, and some proposals try to derive it covariantly from the wave function rather than adding it by hand. ([PMC][1])

So the imaginative move is:

> Maybe Lorentz symmetry is not the deepest rule of reality. Maybe it is the equilibrium-visible rule that emerges when the hidden pilot-wave dynamics cannot be operationally accessed.

That is very Bohmian in spirit.

## The “wrong reading” of special relativity

The possibly wrong reading is:

> “Because no observer can detect absolute simultaneity, absolute simultaneity cannot exist.”

The Bohmian counter-reading would be:

> “Because no observer can detect absolute simultaneity under quantum equilibrium, absolute simultaneity is not part of observable physics — but it might still exist as hidden ontology.”

That is not obviously crazy. It is similar to how Bohmian particles have definite positions even when standard quantum mechanics says the position is not revealed until measurement. The hidden structure is real in the theory, but normally shielded from direct operational access.

Bohmian QFT extensions also make this sharper: instead of treating measurement outcomes as primitive, some models describe particle trajectories, creation and annihilation events, or field configurations guided by the quantum state. ([arXiv][2])

## The deeper possible reinterpretation

Here is the most interesting version:

**Special relativity may govern the public interface of events. Bohmian field theory may require a private coordination layer underneath events.**

In that picture:

* Minkowski spacetime is the stage seen by observers.
* The pilot-wave field or universal wavefunctional is the hidden choreography.
* Lorentz invariance constrains what can be measured and signaled.
* A preferred foliation may coordinate what actually happens beneath the measurement surface.

This would make special relativity somewhat like thermodynamics: completely real, extremely powerful, but possibly not the deepest layer. Temperature is real, but underneath it are molecules. Lorentz symmetry would be real, but underneath it may be a hidden nonlocal ordering principle.

## But the guardrail: this does not overthrow SR

At ordinary quantum equilibrium, Bohmian mechanics is built to reproduce standard quantum predictions. That means no usable faster-than-light signaling, no easy violation of relativistic causality, and no obvious experimental contradiction. The Stanford Encyclopedia notes that Bohmian nonlocality and Lorentz-invariance difficulty are closely connected, but also that orthodox quantum theory has its own tension through collapse-like descriptions. ([Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy][3])

So the sober answer is:

> Bohmian theory does not show that special relativity is wrong. It suggests that the **metaphysical lesson usually extracted from special relativity may be too strong**.

The strongest claim we can responsibly make is not “Einstein was wrong,” but:

> Einstein may have identified the symmetry of observable spacetime, not necessarily the complete ontology of the hidden quantum-real layer.

## A more radical thought experiment

Imagine the universe has two clocks:

The **public clock** is relativistic. It is what observers can compare using light signals, rods, clocks, detectors, and experiments. This clock obeys special relativity.

The **private clock** is Bohmian. It is not an ordinary observable clock. It is the ordering structure required for the universal field configuration to update nonlocally without contradiction.

Ordinary observers only ever see the public clock. But the pilot wave “uses” the private clock to maintain global consistency.

Under this view, Lorentz invariance is not false. It is a **screen symmetry**: the symmetry of what appears inside the empirical interface.

The deeper Bohmian law might be nonlocal, globally coordinated, and possibly foliation-dependent, while still producing Lorentz-invariant observable statistics.

## Where Valentini-style nonequilibrium becomes important

If quantum equilibrium is not universal — for example in the early universe, black holes, high-energy regimes, or pre-Born-rule relic systems — then hidden Bohmian structure might leak into observable physics. Valentini has argued that pilot-wave theory can allow nonequilibrium regimes beyond standard quantum mechanics, potentially producing anomalies in cosmology, Hawking radiation, or short-distance physics. ([arXiv][4])

That is where your idea becomes more testable:

> If SR is only the equilibrium-visible interface, then quantum nonequilibrium might expose where the interface breaks.

This connects directly to your previous Born-rule / equivariance priority. The key is not “break relativity first.” The key is:

> Find whether Born-rule equilibrium is absolute, emergent, or historically contingent.

If equilibrium is absolute, hidden foliation remains metaphysical.
If equilibrium can fail, hidden structure might become empirical.
