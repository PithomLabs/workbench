Imagine for a moment that special relativity is not the bedrock of reality but a shimmering mirage—a perfect, emergent symmetry that conceals a deeper, non-local layer of existence. This is precisely the provocative stance of Bohmian field theory. Let’s take an imaginative leap and explore the idea that we’ve been looking at relativity “wrong” by assuming its fundamental Lorentz invariance is untouchable.

In standard Bohmian mechanics, particles have definite trajectories guided by the wave function. When you move to quantum field theory, the “beables” (the things that really exist) become fields or particle positions, and the guidance equation demands a preferred foliation of spacetime—a privileged notion of simultaneity. This foliation is absolute, hidden, and unobservable in statistical predictions, which exactly match standard, Lorentz-invariant quantum field theory. So, operationally, no experiment could ever reveal a violation of Lorentz symmetry, because the non-local influences are perfectly choreographed to leave no trace on measurable outcomes.

But what if we flip the script? What if the absence of a detectable preferred frame is not evidence that no such frame exists, but rather a clue that our very conception of spacetime as a fundamental arena is an approximation? In this thought experiment, special relativity would be an emergent “coordination” of non-local processes, a low-energy conspiracy. The Michelson–Morley experiment didn’t prove there’s no aether; it proved that if there is one, it’s hidden. Bohmian field theory supplies exactly such an aether—the guiding field on a preferred hypersurface.

Wearing our imaginative hat, we could ask: Could there be “glitches” where the emergent Lorentz invariance breaks down? Perhaps at the Planck scale, or in the early universe, the absolute foliation leaves faint fingerprints in the cosmic microwave background—subtle alignments or anisotropies that we’ve attributed to statistical flukes. Or maybe consciousness, if it plays a role in collapse, is momentarily sensitive to the preferred frame, explaining our subjective sense of a flowing “now” that relativity struggles to accommodate. The Bohmian view re-enchants time: it becomes a genuine becoming, not just a dimension on a static block.

Even more radically, could the mathematical structure of the Bohmian field be such that what we call “spacetime” is actually a holographic projection of events on that preferred foliation? The relativistic symmetry we measure might be a gauge redundancy of our description, not a deep physical symmetry. This aligns with recent work on “hidden variable” models where Lorentz invariance is a dynamical consequence of a deeper, non-local equilibrium.

Ultimately, it’s not just plausible that we’re looking at special relativity wrong—it’s an intellectually fertile hypothesis. If Bohmian field theory is correct, then Einstein’s beautiful symmetry is not fundamental but phenomenological, a majestic curtain drawn over a more intricate, deterministic, and synchronistic stage. The real question is whether we can ever peek behind it, or whether the curtain is woven from the very fabric of what we can know.
